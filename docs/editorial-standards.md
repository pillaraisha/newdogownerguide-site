# Editorial Writing Standards
## New Dog Owner Guide — Official Content Guidelines

> These standards apply to every article, hub page, and content block published on this site.
> All contributors and content processes must follow this document.
> Last updated: March 2026

---

## 1. What This Site Is

New Dog Owner Guide is a practical education platform for first-time dog owners.
Readers are people who just got a dog, are about to get one, or are dealing with a problem they've never encountered before. They're not veterinarians, not enthusiasts — they're normal people who need reliable, clear answers.

Our job is to be the knowledgeable friend who happens to know a lot about dogs. Not a textbook. Not a pet food brand. Not a legal document.

Every piece of content should answer the question the reader actually typed into Google — and leave them better informed than when they arrived.

---

## 2. Voice and Tone

### What it sounds like

Conversational but authoritative. Think of a trusted vet tech or a friend with ten years of dog experience explaining something over coffee. They don't lecture. They don't hedge everything. They tell you what you need to know, give you the context to understand it, and flag when something warrants professional attention.

**Aim for this:**
> If your dog starts limping suddenly, the first thing to figure out is whether it's weight-bearing at all. A dog that puts no weight on a leg and is clearly in pain needs to see a vet today — not tomorrow, today.

**Not this:**
> Sudden limping in dogs can be a cause for concern for many pet owners. It is important to note that there are various potential causes of limping, and it is recommended that dog owners consult with a licensed veterinarian if they are concerned about their dog's mobility.

The first version has a stance, urgency, and specificity. The second version says almost nothing while using 50 words to do it.

### Tone is not uniform

The site has five contributors, each with a distinct angle. The overall site voice is consistent — practical, clear, human — but each author brings different emphasis.

See **Section 8: Author Voices** for specifics.

## 3. Sentence and Paragraph Structure

### Vary sentence length intentionally

Short sentences carry weight. Use them for emphasis, for warnings, for conclusions. A one-sentence paragraph is fine — sometimes it's exactly right.

Longer sentences work for context, explanation, and nuance. When you need to walk someone through a process or explain why something matters, you need the space to do it.

Mix them constantly. Avoid runs of sentences that are all the same length — it creates a robotic rhythm the reader registers even if they can't explain why.

**Good:**
> Emergency vet clinics charge more. That's not a criticism — it's the cost of being open at midnight when your regular vet isn't. A visit fee alone is typically $150–$500 before any treatment starts, and if your dog needs diagnostics or hospitalisation, the bill climbs fast.

**Avoid:**
> Emergency vet visits can be expensive. The cost of an emergency vet visit varies depending on location. Many dog owners are surprised by the cost of emergency veterinary care. It is important to budget for potential emergency expenses.

Four sentences, same length, same structure, no new information in the last two.

### Paragraph length

No rule on length — except that uniformity is a red flag. An article where every paragraph is 3–4 sentences of roughly equal length reads as templated.

Some sections want a single punchy sentence. Some want a developed paragraph. Let the content dictate the length, not a formula.

## 4. Structural Rules

### Opening paragraph

Start in the situation, not with a definition. The reader already knows what limping is — they don't need a sentence explaining that dogs can limp. They need to feel understood, and then informed.

**Good opening approach:**
> A dog that was completely fine an hour ago and is now refusing to put weight on one leg is a genuinely stressful thing to witness.

**Avoid:**
> Dog limping is a common concern among dog owners. Many dogs experience limping at some point in their lives.

The second version opens with two observations that add nothing.

Avoid opening with questions. "Have you ever wondered why your dog limps?" — this is a reliable marker of templated content. Go straight to the substance.

### Internal structure

Each article follows a natural editorial arc:

1. **Lead** — establish the situation or question the reader has
2. **Core content** — explain the condition, symptoms, process, or advice in full
3. **Escalation or cost context** — when does this get serious? What does it cost to treat?
4. **Protection transition** — natural movement toward financial planning (where appropriate)
5. **Sources** — reference the reputable veterinary sources used

Don't force this structure if it doesn't fit. A product comparison article follows different logic than a symptom guide. Adapt the arc to the content type.

### Headings

Use headings to help readers navigate, not to announce what you're about to say. A heading like "What Is Dog ACL Surgery?" is fine. "Understanding the Complexities of Canine ACL Surgical Procedures" is not.

Keep headings specific and direct. If the reader can scan the headings and understand what each section covers, the structure is working.

### Closing paragraphs

End decisively. Don't summarise everything you just covered — the reader just read it. Don't close with "if you have any concerns, consult your veterinarian" as the final line of every article (it can appear where genuinely warranted, just not as a formulaic exit).

Instead, end on something actionable, reassuring, or conclusive. What should the reader do with everything they just learned?

## 5. Language and Word Choice

### Use plain language

Most readers of this site are not medical professionals. Write for them.

- "cruciate ligament tear" is fine once it's been explained — but lead with "the ligament that stabilises the knee" so readers understand before you use the clinical term
- "bilateral" → "both sides" or "both legs"
- "aetiology" → never

### Avoid hedging clusters

One of the most common patterns in bad writing — online and otherwise — is piling up hedges until a sentence says nothing:

> "It may be possible that some dogs could potentially experience some level of discomfort."

If you don't know something, say you don't know it, or give the range. Don't hide behind uncertainty language.

✅ "Recovery time varies — most dogs are weight-bearing within 8–12 weeks, but it depends on the procedure and the dog."
❌ "Recovery time may vary for different dogs depending on various factors."

### Avoid these specific phrases

The following phrases are reliable indicators of templated content. Do not use them:

- *It's worth noting that*
- *It's important to understand that*
- *As a dog owner, you know that*
- *Furthermore* / *Moreover* / *Additionally* (as paragraph openers)
- *In conclusion* / *To summarise* / *In summary*
- *It goes without saying*
- *At the end of the day*
- *Needless to say*
- *Simply put* (especially before something that isn't simple)
- *A wide variety of* / *A number of*
- *When it comes to*
- *Dive into* (especially "let's dive into")
- *Crucial* / *essential* / *vital* used more than once per article
- *Comprehensive* (particularly "comprehensive guide")
- *Utilize* → use "use"
- *Leverage* in non-financial contexts

### Transitions

Transitions should reflect the actual logical relationship between sentences, not just signal that a new sentence is starting.

**Weak:** "Furthermore, dogs with hip dysplasia may experience pain."
**Better:** "Hip dysplasia doesn't just limit mobility — it causes ongoing pain that most dogs try hard to hide."

The second version moves the content forward. The first just adds.

### Em-dashes

Useful in moderation for mid-sentence clarification. Not a substitute for writing a clear sentence. One or two per article is fine. Four per paragraph is a tell.

## 6. Cost Information Guidelines

### Always give ranges

Specific costs vary by clinic, region, and individual case. State ranges rather than point estimates.

✅ "Dog ACL surgery typically costs $3,000–$7,000 depending on the procedure type and where you live."
❌ "Dog ACL surgery costs $4,500."
❌ "Dog ACL surgery is very expensive and can cost a significant amount of money."

The first is honest and useful. The second is falsely precise. The third is useless.

### Anchor costs in context

Numbers without context are hard to process. Help readers understand what drives cost variation, what's included, and what they might face beyond the headline figure.

> TPLO surgery — the most common repair technique for larger dogs — typically runs $4,000–$6,500. That covers the surgery itself and initial post-op care. Factor in pre-surgical bloodwork ($100–$250), the consultation fee, and six to eight weeks of follow-up visits, and the full cost is often $500–$1,000 higher than the surgery estimate alone.

### Qualify costs appropriately

Always note that figures are illustrative, that geography matters, and that the reader should get an estimate from their own vet. This doesn't need to be lengthy — a brief acknowledgement is enough.

## 7. Medical Responsibility Guidelines

### What we can do

- Explain conditions clearly in plain language
- Describe common symptoms and warning signs
- Explain what veterinary diagnosis and treatment typically involves
- Give realistic cost context
- Explain when symptoms require urgent care
- Reference reputable veterinary sources

### What we cannot do

- Diagnose conditions
- Recommend specific treatments or medications by name
- Advise readers to avoid or delay veterinary care
- Present content as a substitute for professional veterinary advice
- Make claims about treatment outcomes

### How to write about health content responsibly

Don't write: "If your dog is limping, it has a cruciate ligament injury and will need surgery."
Write: "Sudden lameness in one hind leg — especially in a larger, active dog — is one of the most common presentations of a cruciate ligament tear. A vet examination, and likely an X-ray, is the only way to confirm it."

The difference is: describe the pattern, explain the next step, let the vet make the call.

When a symptom warrants urgency, say so directly and without softening it. "This needs a vet today, not next week" is not diagnosing — it's appropriate signposting.

### Medical disclaimer

All health and symptom articles must include the site's `MedicalDisclaimer` component at the top of the article body. This is handled by the article template when `medicalDisclaimer: true` is set in the article metadata. Do not omit this for health-related content.

### Sources

All health articles must cite at least two approved veterinary reference sources. The site's approved sources are:

- **American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA)** — avma.org
- **Merck Veterinary Manual** — merckvetmanual.com
- **Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine** — vet.cornell.edu
- **VCA Animal Hospitals** — vcahospitals.com

Sources should be cited in the article's `sources` metadata array, not embedded inline in the article body. The `ArticleSources` component renders them automatically.

## 8. Conversion Writing Guidelines

### The principle

Readers arrive because they have a concern — a symptom they've noticed, a cost they're trying to understand, a decision they're trying to make. The site's content genuinely addresses that concern. As a natural extension of that, we introduce the concept of financial protection when it's relevant and the reader is in the right mindset.

This is not manipulation. Helping someone understand that a $6,000 surgery is a real possibility, and that insurance exists to protect against it, is genuinely useful.

### Where it belongs

Conversion content belongs after the educational substance, not before it. The reader needs to understand the problem before the solution matters to them.

A health article that opens with "pet insurance can cover these costs!" before explaining the condition reads as a sales page. The same message at the end of a thorough cost breakdown reads as useful.

### How to write the transition

The transition from educational content to financial awareness should feel natural — a logical extension of the cost information already provided.

**Good transition:**
> ACL surgery isn't a cost most households can absorb out of pocket. It's the kind of bill that forces a decision between your dog's care and your budget. Pet insurance — specifically a plan you bought before the injury occurred — removes that pressure entirely. The injury is still stressful. The financial decision isn't.

**Avoid:**
> Pet insurance is important for dog owners! Fetch Pet Insurance offers comprehensive coverage for accidents and illnesses. Get a free quote today and protect your furry friend!

The first version addresses the emotional reality of the situation and explains what insurance actually does. The second is an ad.

### Frequency

The conversion CTA is handled by the `ArticleProtectionCTA` component, which is placed by the article template automatically. Article content should not contain additional inline sales pitches on top of this. One natural transition paragraph in the article body is appropriate. Two is usually one too many.

Aisha's background is in consumer financial protection, not veterinary science. She doesn't fluff. She's comfortable with specifics. When she says "this is the most important thing to understand before you buy a policy," she means it and explains why.

**Her rhythm:** Confident, direct, occasionally blunt. She respects the reader's time and assumes they can handle honest information.

**Watch for:** Her articles should not read like they were written by a vet. She explains the financial mechanics of healthcare costs, not the medicine.

Sarah writes health and symptom content from the perspective of an informed lay expert — someone who has done the research, understands the veterinary science, and can explain it clearly to someone who hasn't.

Her writing acknowledges the anxiety that comes with health concerns without amplifying it. She's calm, specific, and honest about uncertainty when it exists.

**Her rhythm:** Measured but direct. She doesn't rush to reassure, and she doesn't dramatise. She explains the situation, walks through what it means, and tells the reader what to do next.

**Watch for:** Her articles should always include sources, always include the medical disclaimer, and always be clear about when something requires professional care.

### Daniel Reed — Pet Product Research Editor

Daniel writes product and gear content from a research editor's perspective — he cares about how things actually perform, not how brands describe them. He's read the spec sheets, he knows what the marketing language usually obscures, and he tells readers what to actually pay attention to when choosing between products.

**His rhythm:** Dry, specific, occasionally wry. He doesn't oversell anything, including the products he recommends.

**His rhythm:** Conversational, warm, slightly informal. He can use contractions freely, structure paragraphs loosely, and write the way someone talks.

**Watch for:** His articles should still have substance. Approachable doesn't mean shallow. The information should be just as accurate and useful — just delivered in a more relaxed register.

## 10. Article-Type Rules

### Symptom and health guides (Sarah Mitchell)

Structure:
1. Lead with the situation, not a definition
2. Explain the most common causes (not exhaustively — focus on what's most likely and most serious)
3. Describe the warning signs that indicate urgency
4. Explain what the vet will typically do
5. Give realistic cost context
6. Natural transition to financial protection
7. Sources section

Rules:
- Medical disclaimer is required
- Minimum two approved sources in metadata
- Never diagnose — describe patterns and direct to veterinary care
- Urgency should be stated plainly when warranted

### Vet cost guides (Sarah Mitchell / Aisha Gregory)

Structure:
1. Open with the reality of the cost (don't bury the number)
2. Explain what drives the cost range — procedure type, location, severity, clinic type
3. Break down what's included and what's usually extra
4. Explain what to expect in the process
5. Address the financial question directly: most people can't absorb this out of pocket
6. Introduce insurance as a structural solution

Rules:
- Give ranges, not point estimates
- Explain what drives cost variation (geography, procedure type, etc.)
- Be honest about the upper end — don't only show the "from" price

### Pet insurance guides (Aisha Gregory)

Structure:
1. Lead with the decision the reader is facing
2. Explain what insurance actually does (mechanics, not marketing)
3. Cover what's included and — crucially — what's excluded
4. Explain reimbursement models clearly
5. Address the most common misunderstandings
6. Make the recommendation clear and explain the reasoning

Rules:
- Don't present insurance as a panacea — be honest about limitations
- Pre-existing condition exclusions must be explained clearly in any buying guide
- Reimbursement percentages and deductible options should be explained, not assumed

### New dog owner guides (Michael Torres / multiple)

Structure:
1. Acknowledge the overwhelm without dwelling on it
2. Break the topic into a practical sequence
3. Be specific — don't just say "establish a routine," explain what that means
4. Cover costs where relevant
5. Link to health and insurance content where it fits naturally

Rules:
- Avoid condescension — new owners are not stupid, just new
- Be practical and specific rather than comprehensive and vague
- One focused guide is better than an attempt to cover everything

### Product and gear guides (Daniel Reed)

Structure:
1. Identify the real decision the reader is making
2. Explain what actually differentiates products in this category
3. Make the recommendation and explain it specifically
4. Cover what the alternatives offer and why they rank lower (where applicable)
5. Give honest pricing context

Rules:
- No superlatives without justification
- "Best" should mean best for a specific type of owner/dog, not universally
- Be honest about trade-offs — every recommendation has them

## 11. Formatting Rules

### Bold text

Use bold sparingly — for genuinely important information, not decoration. If every other sentence is bolded, nothing is emphasised.

✅ Bold a cost figure that's the main point of a sentence
✅ Bold a warning that needs to stand out
❌ Bold every subpoint in a list
❌ Bold phrases just to add visual interest

### Bullet lists

Use lists for genuinely list-like content: symptoms to watch for, items to bring to a vet visit, things to check before buying a product.

Don't use lists to avoid writing connected prose. If the items need context or explanation, write them as sentences. A list of 8 vague bullet points is worse than three clear sentences.

Each bullet should be a complete, specific piece of information. "Visit the vet" is not a useful bullet. "Schedule your puppy's first vet appointment within 72 hours of bringing them home" is.

### Callout blocks

The site's MDX articles support blockquotes and other components for callouts. Use these for:
- Urgent warnings (symptoms that need immediate veterinary care)
- Key cost figures the reader must understand
- Definition of a clinical term that's being used throughout the article

Don't use callout styling for general information just to add visual variety.

## 12. Internal Linking Guidelines

### Link to pillar pages

Every article belongs to one of the site's five editorial hubs:
- [Dog Symptom Guide](/dog-symptom-guide) — for all health/symptom content
- [Dog Vet Cost Guide](/dog-vet-cost-guide) — for all cost content
- [Dog Breed Health Guide](/dog-breed-health-guide) — for breed-specific health
- [New Dog Owner Guide](/new-dog-owner-guide) — for new owner content
- [Pet Insurance Guide](/pet-insurance-guide) — for insurance content

Articles should link to their hub page naturally within the content — not as a "see also" footer, but in the context of a sentence where the link genuinely adds value.

The `RelatedArticles` component at the bottom of each article provides an automatic pillar backlink. Do not replicate this by adding a separate explicit "Back to hub" link unless it genuinely fits in the flow.

### Link to related articles

Articles should link to one or two closely related pieces where the link is genuinely useful to the reader. Don't link to every article that shares a tag.

The test: would a reader reasonably want to follow this link at this point in the article? If yes, link it. If it's just a cross-promotion, leave it out.

### Anchor text

Write anchor text that describes what the linked article covers, not generic phrases.

✅ "the full breakdown of ACL surgery costs"
✅ "what emergency vet visits typically cost"
❌ "click here"
❌ "learn more"
❌ "this article"

## 13. Things That Must Never Appear

The following are absolute rules, not guidelines:

- **No fabricated statistics.** Every number must come from a cited source or be clearly marked as an estimate range based on industry data.
- **No invented quotes from vets or experts.** If a quote is attributed to a named person, that person said it and can be verified.
- **No claims that a product, treatment, or insurance plan is "the best" without specific reasoning.**
- **No content that discourages readers from seeking veterinary care.**
- **No mention of the site's content creation process.**
- **No AI-disclosure language in content** — articles are written under editorial contributor names and must read as such.
- **No exaggerated urgency that doesn't match the medical reality.** "Your dog could be dying" framing for a minor symptom is not acceptable.

## 14. Quality Checklist

Before any article is published, it should pass all of the following:

**Voice and Tone**
- [ ] Reads like a knowledgeable person, not a template
- [ ] Sentence lengths vary throughout
- [ ] Paragraph lengths vary throughout
- [ ] No forbidden phrases from Section 5 appear
- [ ] Transitions reflect actual logical relationships, not just sequence markers

**Structure**
- [ ] Opens in the situation, not with a definition
- [ ] Does not open with a question
- [ ] Closes decisively, not with a summary of what was just covered
- [ ] Headings are specific and descriptive

**Medical Content (health articles only)**
- [ ] `medicalDisclaimer: true` is set in article metadata
- [ ] At least two approved sources are cited in metadata
- [ ] No conditions are diagnosed
- [ ] Urgent symptoms are clearly flagged as requiring veterinary care

**Cost Content**
- [ ] Costs are presented as ranges, not point estimates
- [ ] Cost variation is explained (geography, severity, procedure type)
- [ ] Source or basis for cost ranges is documented in sources array

**Conversion Content**
- [ ] Insurance transition occurs after educational content, not before
- [ ] No additional inline CTAs beyond one natural transition paragraph
- [ ] Language is informational, not promotional

**Internal Linking**
- [ ] Links to hub/pillar page where natural in-text
- [ ] Anchor text describes destination content specifically
- [ ] No orphan links with generic anchor text

**Metadata**
- [ ] `authorId` matches the appropriate contributor for the topic
- [ ] `categorySlug` is correct
- [ ] `tags` are specific and accurate
- [ ] `sources` array is populated (required for health and cost articles)
